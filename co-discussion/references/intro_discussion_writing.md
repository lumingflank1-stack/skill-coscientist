# Introduction and Discussion Writing

## Intake

Before drafting, identify:

- One-sentence manuscript argument.
- Core Results claims.
- Evidence supporting each claim.
- Boundary of the claim.
- Target journal or audience, if provided.
- Terms that must remain consistent across sections.

If the core claim, evidence, or boundary is missing, write a scaffold and a missing-input note.

## Introduction Structure

Use:

`field scale -> bottleneck -> prior work -> unresolved gap -> present study`

Typical paragraph jobs:

1. Establish why the disease, process, or model matters.
2. Define the bottleneck or unresolved mechanism.
3. Synthesize prior attempts and what they do not explain.
4. State what this study does and preview the approach.

Do not summarize numeric Results in the Introduction.

## Discussion Structure

Use:

`central advance -> evidence meaning -> relation to prior work -> rival explanations -> limitations -> future work`

Discussion should interpret rather than repeat Results figure by figure.

Include:

- What the findings suggest.
- How they align or conflict with prior literature.
- Alternative explanations.
- Specific limitations from this study design.
- What would be needed for causal or translational claims.

## Claim Discipline

- Use `show` or `demonstrate` only for direct evidence.
- Use `suggest`, `indicate`, `is consistent with`, `may`, or `could` for indirect or computational evidence.
- Do not claim novelty beyond the searched literature.
- Do not remove provenance. If a result is virtual, keep that status in the report or source ledger; the manuscript-facing Discussion can otherwise use conventional Results-based prose.

## Output Shape

Save:

- `introduction.md`
- `discussion.md`
- `limitations_and_alternatives.md`
- `claim_evidence_citation_map.csv`
